Salma Noor Mahomed is a South African IT professional and writer based in Berlin. She graduated cum laude from the University of Cape Town with degrees in Media & Writing and Information Systems. Having experience in technical writing and teaching programming, she excels in areas of research, collaboration, and creative problem solving. With the skills she has gained in her professional and personal life, she aims to establish a foundation for herself in research and academia. She is primarily interested in topics around identity, culture, and decolonisation especially in Islamic and African contexts.

I was pretty young when I realised I enjoy writing. It was one thing getting good marks in school for the essays I was writing in English class and history and art theory, but it was an entire other thing to come home from school and write blog posts about a poem I recently read or a movie I recently watched. Obviously nobody read those posts and actually most of them stayed in the Blogger drafts (the ones that made the cut are still here in the web space). But the process of writing about something and adding a photograph to complement it brought me a type of joy I didn't feel in many things I did. As I got older, I did all the things that I thought would make me a writer: I joined the university newspaper, I did an arts degree, took some English literature courses and even majored in writing, but I never followed through on becoming a writer. As in, sitting down and writing something that can go somewhere and be its own thing. Besides for university essays obviously. And maybe when I worked as a technical writer at a software company (but I don't think that counts at all).

What I did instead is what many do when faced with the reality of their life and the cards they've been dealt: try to prove their aunty wrong said that a woman with an arts degree needs to marry a rich man. I was 18 and I took that to heart because unfortunately I did want to be rich but did not want to marry a man. So I went down the STEM route. I noticed that there were so few women in my Information Systems classes, and I thought that more of them ought to listen to their aunties. But I found that besides knowing I'll be rich working in IT, I loved learning about systems, how we create them and use them. It made sense to me. I loved building things with code and solving problems and getting programs to run. In this time I also learnt that I really liked the academic environment: reading, learning, writing, collaborating. I didn't continue studying a Master's programme in my country because it's expensive and I was tired of working to afford studying and I really wanted to finally be rich and prove my aunty wrong.

A while ago I found the Design and Computation programme but I didn't think it was possible to even apply because I thought the whole programme was taught in German. Turns out it isn't, and it also turns out that learning German isn't so bad so I'm doing that regardless. I want to study this Master's because I still don't really know where my niche lies and that seems to be a good thing for this programme. I didn't become a writer nor a developer nor did I become rich. That's all fine though. I want a space where I can learn new things and write new things again. I guess this doesn't answer some important questions like what I could contribute to this programme and why I could be a valuable addition to the cohort. I hope this portfolio does that instead. Sorry, maybe you didn't have to read this part at all..?

When I was 11 I was given unrestricted access to the internet (hence my Blogger era) and I downloaded Instagram and actually thrived on the platform.
